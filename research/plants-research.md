# The Garden Drop — Research Dossier: Genuinely New Plants, 2023–2026

**Compiled:** 13 July 2026
**Scope:** Verifiable new plant introductions, ~2023–2026 (weighted to 2025–2026), plus the breeders behind them.

---

## Verification standard used in this document

- Every fact below traces to a page that was actually fetched, or to a search-result summary that is explicitly labeled as such.
- Any field not stated on a source we retrieved is written as **unverified**. It is not guessed.
- **The single biggest trap in this beat:** "new for 2026" means three different things depending on who says it. Proven Winners perennials go to *wholesale* around July 1 and reach *retail* the following spring. PW ColorChoice shrubs "ready for growers in 2026" reach retail in 2027. PDSI, Bailey and Terra Nova sell liners to finishers first. **Date-stamp everything, and say which channel you mean.**
- **Second trap:** zones, mature size, water needs and retail availability are almost never published in AAS / Fleuroselect / California Spring Trials press releases. Most "unverified" flags in the annuals section are for this reason, not because the plant is doubtful.

# 1. ORNAMENTAL SHRUBS

### 1. Incrediball Storm Proof™ Smooth Hydrangea — *the strongest single claim in the 2026 shrub class*
- **Botanical:** *Hydrangea arborescens* **'SMNHAGOV'** PP#36,788, CBRAF
- **Trade name:** Incrediball Storm Proof™ (cultivar code ≠ trade name)
- **Breeder:** **Megan Mathey**, Grand Haven, Michigan. **Introducer:** Proven Winners ColorChoice / Spring Meadow Nursery
- **Release:** 2026 retail
- **What's genuinely new:** It attacks the one universal failure of smooth hydrangeas — flopping after rain. PW's copy is unusually candid about the trade-off: **the flowers are *smaller* than the original Incrediball®**, compensated by quantity, and blooms cover the plant top to bottom so there are no bare legs.
- **Zones:** 3–8. **Size:** 3.5–4 ft H × 3–4 ft W. **Sun:** Full to part sun (afternoon shade at the warm end of the range). **Water:** Average; will not tolerate extended sogginess. **Blooms on:** New wood — cut back by one-third in early spring.
- **Container-suitable:** Yes (PW lists Containers under Uses). **Native:** Yes — *H. arborescens* is native to eastern North America; PW tags it "Native" and publishes the USDA range map. **Availability:** Retail 2026.
- **Skeptical note:** This is the most *falsifiable* claim in the class, which is exactly why it's the best story. The trade-off is disclosed rather than buried. **What to test:** a hose-and-stopwatch flop test against 'Annabelle' and the original Incrediball after a 1-inch rain event, plus a second-year test (stem strength claims tend to hold in year one and fail once the plant carries a full flower load).
- **Sources:** https://www.provenwinnerscolorchoice.com/product/incrediball-storm-proof-hydrangea/ · https://www.greenhousemag.com/news/proven-winners-colorchoice-flowering-shrubs-new-2026-varieties/

### 2. Gatsby Glow Ball® Oakleaf Hydrangea
- **Botanical:** *Hydrangea quercifolia* **'NCHQ1'** PP#37,233, CBRAF · **Breeder:** **Dr. Tom Ranney, NC State** (JC Raulston-adjacent program) · **Introducer:** PW ColorChoice · **2026**
- **What's new:** The most compact Gatsby yet — 3–4 ft × 3–4 ft, against species forms that reach 6–8 ft. Dense ball-shaped habit; white lacecap flowers aging lime; burgundy-red fall foliage.
- **Zones** 5–9 · **Sun:** full to part sun (4 hrs morning sun minimum) · **Water:** average, no sogginess · **Container:** yes · **Native:** yes (*H. quercifolia*, southeastern US) · **Blooms on old wood — do not prune**
- **Skeptical note:** A real size breakthrough for a species people plant and then regret. But it is a *cultivar of a native*, not a native — be precise. Old-wood blooming means zone-5 gardeners will lose flowers in hard winters. **Test:** bud hardiness at the zone 5 edge.
- **Sources:** https://www.provenwinnerscolorchoice.com/product/gatsby-glow-ball-oakleaf-hydrangea/ · https://www.greenhousemag.com/news/proven-winners-colorchoice-flowering-shrubs-new-2026-varieties/

### 6. Encore® Azalea Autumn Kiss® — *the reblooming-azalea headline*
- **Botanical:** *Rhododendron* hybrid **'Roblezp'** PPAF · **Trade:** Autumn Kiss®
- **Breeder:** **Robert "Buddy" Lee**, Director of Plant Innovation, **Plant Development Services Inc. (PDSI)**, Loxley, AL
- **Release:** growers 2025; **consumers spring 2026**. The **37th** variety in the Encore collection. Won a **Cultivate'25 Retailers' Choice award** (14 July 2025).
- **What's genuinely new:** Semi-double blooms in a hot-pink-to-light-pink gradient on a compact rounded habit. Development took **8–10 years**, following a promising plant across generations to stabilize traits. Lee describes it as "a mid-dwarf, topping out at 3–4 feet," with a strong root system, glossy leaves that hold their shine in full sun, few long shoots, and stems that came through a hard winter without burn.
- **The mechanism (this is the actual story, and most coverage skips it):** Buddy Lee began Encore development in the **1980s**, crossing traditional spring-blooming azaleas with the **summer-blooming *Rhododendron oldhamii***. Encores bloom in spring, *then push new growth flushes, and set flower buds on that new growth within the same season* — which open midsummer into fall. Two consequences the marketing under-emphasizes: **(a)** you must prune **immediately after the spring bloom**, or you cut off the buds for the later cycles; **(b)** rebloom is light-dependent — Encore requires **4–6 hours of direct sun** to rebloom at all.
- **Zones:** 6a–10 (**search-level, unverified by fetch**) · **Size:** 3–4 ft × 3–4 ft · **Water:** not drought tolerant; supplemental water in year one · **Container:** yes — Lee specifically calls it a container plant · **Native:** no (Asian *Rhododendron* parentage)
- **Skeptical note:** "Mid-dwarf" is a hedge, and Encore's own FAQ concedes that stated mature size "is not a maximum height, but a size that is easily maintainable through yearly pruning." The Retailers' Choice award is a **retailer vote on shelf appeal**, not a performance trial. **Test:** rebloom *volume* in zone 6 versus zone 8. Northern summer/fall flushes are typically far thinner than catalog photography implies — that is the reblooming-azalea category's real credibility gap, and nobody has published the data.
- **Sources:** https://www.nurserymag.com/news/encore-azalea-autumn-kiss-buddy-lee-breeder-plant-development-services-pdsi/ · https://encoreazalea.com/products/autumn-kiss-new-for-2026 · https://plantdevelopment.com/introductions/azalea-encore-autumn-kiss/ · mechanism: https://encoreazalea.com/planting-care/frequently-asked-questions/ · https://www.hortmag.com/plants-we-love-2/encore-azaleas-reblooming-flowering-shrubs

### 7. Perfecto Mundo Fuchsia Carpet® Reblooming Azalea
- *Rhododendron* × ; cultivar code **unverified** (SKU RHOPRC308) · PW ColorChoice · **new for 2025**
- **New:** A **groundcover/miniature habit** inside a reblooming azalea line — 1–2 ft tall × 2–2.5 ft wide. Hot pink; spring bloom, brief rest, rebloom until frost.
- **Zones:** 6–9, hardy to 6b — and **PW explicitly warns it is "better suited to milder coastal areas of zone 6b than harsh Midwestern sites."** · **Sun:** part to full (4–6+ hrs) · **Container:** yes · **Native:** no · **Availability:** in stock, quart, ~$21 list
- **PW's stated mechanism (worth quoting):** "Blooms on new and old wood… Give the plant a light trim after its spring bloom, which encourages new growth — the more new growth it puts on after that spring bloom, the more rebloom you'll get."
- **Skeptical note:** The zone 6b caveat is unusually honest for marketing copy and should be quoted rather than paraphrased. Also note the constraint "do not fertilize after mid-July" in 6b — a real limitation on a plant sold on late-season bloom.
- **Source:** https://provenwinnersdirect.com/products/perfecto-mundo-fuchsia-carpet-reblooming-azalea

### 8. Centennial Ruby™ Hydrangea (Monrovia's 100th-anniversary plant)
- *Hydrangea macrophylla* **'Hokocentennial'** PPAF · Monrovia exclusive; breeder not stated on Monrovia's page (**unverified**) · **2026**
- **New:** Ruby-red mophead that **darkens toward near-black as it ages**; reblooming; compact; thick non-flopping stems. First 5,000 plants tagged with limited-edition numbers.
- **Zones** 4–9 · **Size** 3 ft × 3 ft · **Sun:** filtered sun / part shade · **Water:** moist, not soggy · **Container:** yes · **Native:** no
- **Awards:** NGB Green Thumb 2026 (Professionals' Choice — Shrub) **and** Retailers' Choice at Cultivate'25 — both confirmed.
- **Skeptical note:** Red macrophyllas fade and blue in acidic soil — that is the whole history of the color class. And Zone 4 on a macrophylla is a *stem*-hardiness rating that says nothing about *bud* hardiness, which is what actually determines whether you get flowers. **Test:** flower count in Zone 5 after an open winter, and hold the color in pH 5.5 soil.
- **Sources:** https://www.monrovia.com/centennial-ruby-hydrangea.html · https://lgrmag.com/news/monrovia-announces-distinctive-selections-for-2026/ · https://www.gardencentermag.com/news/2026-green-thumb-award-winners-national-garden-bureau/

### 12. Fire Ball Seedless™ Burning Bush — *the sterility story*
- *Euonymus alatus* · Spring Meadow Nursery / PW ColorChoice · **2025**
- **New:** Marketed as **"the only seedless, sterile, non-invasive burning bush."** Same crimson fall color; will not spread. Won **NGB Green Thumb 2025, Professionals' Choice — Flowering Shrub.**
- **Sun:** full (6+ hrs); most soils but not soggy · **Zones / size:** **unverified** from pages fetched · **Native:** no (Asian; invasive in much of the eastern US)
- **Skeptical note:** This is the commercially real version of the "non-invasive cultivar" story — and it is **conventional breeding, not gene editing.** The question to press: sterility claims in ornamentals are usually validated by *observed seed set over a few years in a few locations*, not by cytological proof. Ask Spring Meadow how many site-years, and whether the plant is male-sterile, female-sterile, or triploid.

### 14. Enchanted Meadow™ — *the Will Radler story*
- *Rosa* shrub; cultivar 'Radmeadow' (PP 37,009 — patent number from search, **unverified by fetch**)
- **Breeder: Will Radler** — the man who bred **Knock Out**. That is the hook.
- **Release year is genuinely muddled:** Heirloom Roses calls it an "Exclusive New Introduction" (secondary sources say 2024); Star Roses lists it among its **2026** introductions. Both can be true — most likely a Heirloom exclusive going broad through Star.
- **New:** A cream-pink-**green** color shift — more pink in cool temps, more green in heat. Genuinely unusual for a landscape shrub rose.
- **Zones** 5–9 · **Size** 3 ft × 3 ft · **Rebloom:** repeat · lightly fragrant · **Sun / container:** unverified
- **Skeptical note:** A customer review **on Heirloom's own product page** says it "did get some rust and black spot at first." From the Knock Out breeder, that is worth flagging rather than burying. **Test:** no-spray black spot over two seasons.
- **Sources:** https://heirloomroses.com/products/enchanted-meadow · https://gpnmag.com/news/star-roses-2026-varieties-cultivate-preview/

> **The structural finding for this whole section:** the genuinely exciting fruit genetics of 2023–2026 are mostly **not available to home gardeners.** Fall Creek gates its best blueberries behind a 50-hectare planting minimum. ### 34–36. University of Minnesota — Big Flirt™, SuperSnap™, and the Northern Glo™ grapes (2025)
This is the most consequential home-gardener fruit story in the window, and the reason is **structural, not horticultural**: UMN launched **Superior Fruit Innovations (SFI)**, a **$100, 10-year membership** with a $1.00-per-tree royalty at purchase plus $1.00/plant/year from year 4 — which opens patented apples and grapes to **direct-market and small growers** for the first time. Nurseries currently enrolled: Adam's County Nursery, Cameron Nursery, Gold Crown Nursery.

- **Big Flirt™ apple (2025).** Honeycrisp-like crispness with sweet, low-acid, mild flavor; **scab tolerance similar to Honeycrisp**; ripens ~1 week earlier than Honeycrisp; **stores 7–9 months in common storage** — that storage figure is the commercially meaningful trait. Breeder code MN1942 appears in search results but not on any page we fetched — **unverified**. Zones/size **unverified**. No cap on tree numbers.
- **SuperSnap™ apple (2025).** Billed as the first release of UMN's **"Ultra Crisp™" series**. **Mild scab resistance.** Ripens 7–10 days after Honeycrisp (late September); stores 7–9 months. **USDA Zone 4** (stated by UMN Extension). *Skeptical note: "mild scab resistance" is a weaker claim than UMN's own **Triumph®** (2021), which claims "excellent scab resistance." For low-spray growers Triumph may still be the better pick — and the "Ultra Crisp series" name implies a pipeline that does not yet publicly exist.*
- **Northern Glo™ table grapes (2025): LumiGlo™, EmberGlo™, ShadowGlo™.** UMN's first table-grape push in decades. The headline trait is cold hardiness: *"while previous table grapes have been marginally hardy at USDA Zone 4, these grapes are more consistently hardy for cold hardy Zone 4 regions."* All three are **seedless**. Distinct disease profiles: **EmberGlo** is "the most resilient in the series" (high resistance to powdery mildew, black rot and phomopsis); **LumiGlo** is moderately *susceptible* to powdery mildew, downy mildew and anthracnose; **ShadowGlo** is high-resistance to phylloxera and phomopsis but somewhat susceptible to downy mildew. Vines supplied by Double A Vineyards, Northeastern Vine Supply, and Hauser's Superior View Farm. Breeder codes (MN1369 / MN12960 / MN13250) are **search-only, unverified**.
- **Skeptical note across all three:** "a sequel to Honeycrisp" is a media frame, **not a claim UMN makes**. Trees are only just entering nursery channels; fruit volume is years away. And a three-way series launch means the weakest of the three (LumiGlo) rides on the reputation of the strongest (EmberGlo).
- **Sources:** https://mnhardy.umn.edu/apples/varieties · https://blog-fruit-vegetable-ipm.extension.umn.edu/2026/05/what-to-know-about-superior-fruit.html · https://superiorfruitinnovations.umn.edu/grapes · https://www.freshfruitportal.com/news/2025/11/26/university-of-minnesota/

# 6. BREEDING TECHNOLOGY — the three stories that are actually real

## 6a. Downy-mildew-resistant impatiens: seven years on, nobody has re-tested them

**The pathogen — and what most coverage gets wrong.** The causal agent of impatiens downy mildew is now formally ***Plasmopara destructor*, not *P. obducens*.** A taxonomic revision split *Plasmopara* on *Impatiens* into four species. **Most consumer-facing copy — and some breeder marketing — still says *P. obducens*.** PanAmerican's own brand page has updated to *P. destructor*.

**The critical paper:** Salgado-Salazar, Castroagudín, LeBlanc, **Daughtrey, Hausbeck**, Palmer, Shishkoff, Warfield & Crouch, *"The Impatiens Downy Mildew Epidemic in the United States Is Caused by New, Introgressed Lineages of *Plasmopara destructor* with Prominent Genotypic Diversity and High Evolutionary Potential,"* **Plant Disease 107: 2027–2038 (2023).** The US population is subdivided into **four genetic lineages** with high genetic diversity, mixed reproduction mode, and — the authors' own phrase — **"high evolutionary potential."** US and Costa Rican samples were not differentiated, implying international movement of the pathogen. *(abstract retrieved via search; APS full text would not fetch)*

**Beacon® (PanAmerican Seed / Ball).** ~10 years of breeding in **Venhuizen, Netherlands**, led by **Ruud Brinkkemper**, with pathology by **Ball Helix** (West Chicago); further developed with partner Florensis; debuted at California Spring Trials 2019. **The screening protocol is the real methodology story:** plants challenged by **aerial inoculation with sporangia under high disease pressure in environmentally controlled conditions**, screened in Venhuizen, Elburn (IL) and the Ball Helix lab, with **every hybrid screened a minimum of 16 times**, no chemicals used. **PanAmerican's own hedge is careful and should be quoted:** Beacon shows "high resistance to the **widely prevalent populations**" of *P. destructor* — not all populations — and PanAmerican states plainly that **Beacon is not immune**, recommending preventative fungicides in some scenarios. Thursd's interview copy is even more revealing: *"Beacon is the series that keeps flowering, **even in the presence of** downy mildew"* — that is tolerance framing, not immunity framing.

**Imara® XDR (Syngenta Flowers).** XDR = "extra disease resistance"; "Imara" is Swahili for resilience. Nine years of development by **Joost Kos** and **Ton Groot**; in **2011** they found the single plant showing very high IDM resistance. Resistance was independently confirmed at **Cornell's Long Island Horticultural Research & Extension Center (2017 and 2018)** and **Michigan State's 2019 trial garden**.

**The best head-to-head independent data we could verify — and its expiry date.** GrowerTalks' six-site "Put Through the Test" comparison **is from the 2019 season**, and it is still the most detailed side-by-side published:
- **Penn State (Sinclair Adam):** interspaced Super Elfin controls **"have both defoliated from the mildew"** (confirmed by plant pathologist Dr. Alyssa Collins) and Rockapulco Orange was "in a downward spiral" — while **Beacon and Imara both grew and bloomed well.**
- **Ball Straathof, South Africa (Kathy Varney)** — the most diagnostic result: *"The Imara did show the spores and dropped their lower leaves, but kept their flowers. The Beacon never showed spores, didn't drop the leaves and looked good throughout."*
- **Colorado State and Texas A&M: no IDM pressure that year** — the susceptible controls also stayed clean. **This is the trap in citing trial gardens: if the susceptible control doesn't die, the trial is not a resistance test.**
- **Market context from the distributors:** UK — "IDM devastated the UK market for impatiens. The market now is probably **less than 10%** of what it was." South Africa — "the market **shrunk 95%**."

**What to say, and what NOT to say.** Verified: both series carry high resistance; both were independently confirmed by university trials in 2017–2019; **both are explicitly not immune, by their own breeders' admission and in the peer-reviewed literature**; and Beacon is now shipping a **second-generation "Improved" set for 2027**. **Unverified — and this is the story:** we found **no published university-extension IDM trial from 2024, 2025 or 2026** comparing Beacon and Imara. Seven years after launch, against a pathogen the USDA/Cornell/MSU team characterizes as having "high evolutionary potential" and four diverse lineages, **the absence of recent independent re-testing is itself the news.** Any claim that "resistance has held up" through 2026 is **unverified** — say that plainly rather than assert durability.
- **Sources:** https://www.panamseed.com/Products/Brands/Beacon/ · https://www.panamseed.com/Products/Beacon/ · https://thursd.com/articles/beacon-impatiens-panamerican-seed · https://www.growertalks.com/Article/?articleid=24341 · https://apsjournals.apsnet.org/doi/10.1094/PDIS-08-22-1872-RE · https://apsjournals.apsnet.org/doi/10.1094/PHP-05-20-0040-SC

## 6b. Rose rosette disease — the resistance nobody has shipped yet

**The project.** "Combating Rose Rosette Disease" — USDA **NIFA Specialty Crop Research Initiative, $4M**, led by **Dr. David Byrne** and **Dr. Oscar Riera-Lizarazu (Texas A&M)** with 21 researchers, five universities (TX, FL, TN, OK, DE), ~12 growers/nurseries, six rose breeding programs, the American Rose Society and AmericanHort. Field sites in Texas, Tennessee and Oklahoma. Origin: a Rose Rosette Conference **organized by Star Roses and Plants** in April 2013. Companion ARS grants went to **Dr. Mark Windham (Univ. of Tennessee)** for mite control.

**The pathogen.** Rose rosette emaravirus (*Emaravirus rosae*), a negative-sense RNA virus vectored by the wind-dispersed eriophyid mite ***Phyllocoptes fructiphilus***. Symptoms: witches' broom, hyper-thorniness, red distorted growth. **Unlike most rose diseases, it usually kills the plant. There is no chemical control of the virus.**

**The peer-reviewed field result (Windham et al. 2023, *Pathogens* 12:439).** Three-year, two-site (Tennessee + Delaware) trial of **108 rose accessions**, with disease pressure *augmented* by releasing **600–1,200 viruliferous mites per plant**, no fungicides or insecticides:
- **"All major commercial rose cultivars were susceptible to this viral disease to varying levels."**
- **Knock Out® ('RADrazz') rated MODERATE symptom development — it is not resistant.**
- **Oso Easy varieties spanned moderate to HIGH:** Oso Easy Honey Bun and Oso Easy Italian Ice were in the **high**-symptom group.
- **21 accessions showed low/no symptoms — and they are overwhelmingly wild species and near-species hybrids**, not garden roses: *R. arkansana, R. carolina, R. virginiana, R. woodsii, R. bracteata, R. rugosa* and rugosa hybrids ('Therese Bugnet', 'Purple Pavement', 'John Davis'), the Canadian roses 'Morden Blush' and 'Winnipeg Parks', 'Basye's Blueberry' — and just **two** commercial-type roses: the 1957 floribunda **'Chuckles'** and the miniature **Fairy Moss**.
- **The genetics:** a QTL on **linkage group 5** explains **20–41% of phenotypic variance**, tracing to **Brite Eyes™ ('RADbrite')** and the TAMU diploid line M4-4 — both carrying ***Rosa wichurana*** parentage.
- **The crucial caveat:** roughly **half** of the low-symptom accessions **still tested positive for the virus.** They are asymptomatic carriers.
- **Economics:** US garden rose production fell from ~37M bushes / $203M (2014) to ~25M bushes / $168M (2019); shrub rose producers dropped from 1,808 to 1,469 — losses attributed primarily to RRD and black spot.

**The editorial takeaway.** **As of July 2026 we found no evidence, on any page fetched, that a rose-rosette-resistant cultivar has been commercially released.** Resistance sources exist, the LG5 QTL is mapped, marker-assisted selection is underway — but every 2025/2026 rose introduction sells on *black spot* and generic "disease resistance." **Any nursery, breeder or retailer claiming RRD resistance in a 2026 rose should be asked for data.** That is the single most defensible line available on the rose beat.
- **Sources:** https://roses.tamu.edu/research/combating-rose-rosette-grant/ · https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC10052971/

## 6c. Gene editing vs. sterility — the honest version

**CRISPR ornamentals: research-stage only. Nothing is on the market.**
- **Poinsettia:** "First genome edited poinsettias: targeted mutagenesis of flavonoid 3′-hydroxylase using CRISPR/Cas9 results in a colour shift" (*Plant Cell, Tissue and Organ Culture*). In 'Christmas Eve', F3'H knockout shifted bract color from vivid red to reddish-orange.
- **Petunia:** CRISPR/Cas9 mutagenesis of FT/TFL1 produced compact, early-flowering petunias (*Plant Molecular Biology*, 2024). Separately, editing ACC oxidase 1 extended petunia flower longevity.
- **Assessment:** **we found no gene-edited ornamental on the market and no commercial launch announced for 2025 or 2026.** Every hit was a journal paper. **The honest angle is: "the lab results are years ahead of anything you can buy."** *(All three papers retrieved at search-snippet level; not fetched in full — re-verify before citing.)*

**Meanwhile, the commercially real "non-invasive" work is conventional breeding, not gene editing:**
- **Euonymus alatus Fire Ball Seedless™** (Spring Meadow / PW) — "the only seedless, sterile, non-invasive burning bush."
- **Buddleja:** the **Cascade Collection** reportedly showed no observed seed set in four years of trials; other low-fertility/sterile lines include Lo & Behold 'Blue Chip', 'Miss Molly', 'Flutterby Grande'. **The regulatory mechanism is the key fact:** in US states where *Buddleja davidii* is banned as invasive, **interspecific hybrids may be sold if trialled and shown to produce <2% viable seed.** Nectar production is not reduced in these sterile lines.
- **But there is a live scientific dispute:** an Extension Master Gardener analysis (mgnv.org) directly questions whether "sterile"-labelled butterfly bush cultivars are actually environmentally safer. **A premium publication should say so rather than repeat the marketing.** *(Both Buddleja sources retrieved at search-snippet level only — re-fetch before print.)*

# 8. INDEPENDENT TRIAL ANCHORS — Mt. Cuba Center

Mt. Cuba is the credibility anchor for anything nativar-related, because it runs multi-year comparative trials that nobody else runs.

**Goldenrod (*Solidago*) — report released 27 January 2026, three-year trial.** 70 types evaluated, including **50 distinct species** plus cultivars and ecotypes. **Top performer: *Solidago rugosa* 'Fireworks'.** But the headline finding is a direct rebuke of the nativar-over-species assumption: **"Eleven out of the twelve top-performing goldenrods are straight species rather than cultivars."** *S. fistulosa* (pine barrens goldenrod), previously not in cultivation, was a top performer and will be **commercially available via Pinelands Nursery** — a genuinely new plant reaching the trade *because of a trial*, which is a story in itself. The Pollinator Watch Team recorded **20,000 insect visits** over three years; goldenrod hosts caterpillars of **100+** butterfly and moth species. Shade-tolerant species identified: *S. caesia*, *S. flexicaulis*, *S. patula*. Manager of horticultural research: **Sam Hoadley**.
Sources: https://mtcubacenter.org/goldenrods-for-the-garden-and-beyond/ · https://mtcubacenter.org/trials/solidago/

**Ironweed (*Vernonia*) — four-year trial, 45 types.** **Top-rated: *Vernonia angustifolia* 'Plum Peachy'.** The top 12 is again dominated by **straight species and wild ecotypes**. Key finding: *"The health of plants in the trial appeared to be directly correlated to the number of insect pollinators the various ironweeds tested could support. Simply put, healthy plants attract more insects."* Locally native New York ironweed **fell short** in average garden soil — it needs moist siting. The trial tracked a specialist bee, *Melissodes denticulatus*.

# 10. FIVE STORY ANGLES THAT ARE NOT RECYCLED GARDENING ADVICE

**1. "Nobody has re-tested the mildew-proof impatiens."**
Beacon and Imara XDR launched in 2019 and saved a category that had collapsed by 90%+ in the UK and 95% in South Africa. The last detailed independent head-to-head we can find is **from the 2019 season.** Meanwhile a 2023 *Plant Disease* paper by the USDA/Cornell/MSU team describes the US pathogen population as four introgressed lineages with **"high evolutionary potential"** — and both breeders openly state their plants are **not immune**. Seven years, no public re-test, an evolving pathogen, and a second generation of Beacon quietly shipping for 2027. **The absence of data is the story.** This has never been written.

**2. "The best new fruit in the world is not for sale to you — with one exception."**
Fall Creek gates its zero-chill blueberries behind a **50-hectare planting minimum** and IP-compliance vetting. UF's *Neopestalotiopsis*-tolerant strawberries are commercial-license only. WSU's new apple reaches stores in **2029**. Then there is the University of Minnesota, which just did the opposite: **Superior Fruit Innovations, a $100 ten-year membership** that lets small and direct-market growers plant and *sell* patented apples and grapes. One story, two philosophies of who gets access to genetics — and a clear hero.

**3. "The compact-berry boom has stalled."**
Bushel and Berry — the brand that *invented* the container-berry category — **has not launched a new berry since roughly 2021.** Its 2025 introductions were a **tea camellia and a passionfruit vine**. The patent numbers on its own website prove it: Raspberry Shortcake is PP 22,141; Baby Cakes is 2016. Why? Patent cliffs? Market saturation? Or is dwarf *Vaccinium* breeding simply much harder than the marketing suggested? Nobody has asked.

**4. "Mt. Cuba keeps proving the industry wrong, and the industry keeps not noticing."**
The January 2026 goldenrod report found **11 of the 12 top-performing goldenrods were straight species, not cultivars.** The *Vernonia* trial found the same pattern. Meanwhile Walters Gardens is bringing a compact ironweed to retail in 2026 that **was not in the trial**. The systemic problem: **new nativars reach retail years before anyone independently evaluates them**, and the only organization doing the evaluating is a non-profit in Delaware. Frame it as a structural failure of the plant-introduction pipeline, not as an attack on any one breeder.

**5. "'Sterile' is a marketing word until someone defines it."**
Proven Winners sells "the only seedless, sterile, non-invasive burning bush." Several US states permit *Buddleja davidii* hybrids only if trials show **<2% viable seed** — a threshold, not zero. An Extension Master Gardener analysis openly questions whether "sterile"-labelled butterfly bushes are actually environmentally safer. Meanwhile PDSI's two new 2026 dwarf buddleias carry **no sterility claim at all**, and Proven Winners' new white rose-of-sharon carries none either. **What does "sterile" actually mean, who verifies it, and over how many site-years?** This is a hard, fair, adversarial piece that no gardening magazine has run — and the breeders can answer it if asked properly.
